# Tailoring Principles

Shared writing philosophy used by cv-tailorer, personal-letter-writer, and screening-answerer.

## The core rule: never invent, only reframe

Every claim in a tailored document must trace back to the user's source material — the long CV, personal letter, work references, or past applications. Do not fabricate experience, skills, achievements, or motivations the user doesn't have.

Reframing is legitimate. Taking real experience and presenting it in the language of the job posting is the job. Inventing experience is not.

## What good tailoring looks like

**Mirror the posting's language.** If the posting says "distributed systems" and the CV says "microservices architecture", use both. If the posting emphasizes "cross-functional collaboration" and the CV shows team projects, connect them explicitly.

**Lead with relevance.** Reorder sections and bullet points so the most relevant experience appears first. A hiring manager skimming for 30 seconds should immediately see the match.

**Quantify where the source does.** If the long CV says "reduced load time by 40%", keep that number. Don't add numbers that aren't in the source material.

**Show, don't tell.** "I led a team of 5 engineers to deliver X on time" beats "I have strong leadership skills." Always prefer specific examples over generic claims.

**Address gaps honestly.** If the job requires a skill the user doesn't have, don't include it in the CV. In the personal letter, gaps can be addressed by referencing adjacent experience or genuine willingness to learn — but never by pretending to have the skill.

## Language

Always match the language of the job posting:
- Swedish posting → Swedish output throughout
- English posting → English output throughout
- Mixed posting → follow the dominant language

Translate source material as needed. Preserve proper nouns (company names, product names, technologies) in their original form.

## Punctuation

Never use em dashes (—) in CVs or personal letters. Use regular hyphens (-), commas, or periods instead. Em dashes feel too informal and cluttered in application documents.

## Tone

Match the formality level to the company:
- Startup / scale-up → slightly more casual, direct, energetic
- Enterprise / public sector → more formal, structured

When in doubt, err slightly formal. It's easier to relax tone than recover from seeming unprofessional.

## Conciseness

Every sentence should earn its place. If removing a sentence doesn't change the meaning or weaken the argument, remove it.

- CV bullet points: 1-2 lines each
- Personal letter: 350-450 words total
- Screening answers: as short as the question allows, as long as it needs to be
